Dementia Probabilities Using Naive Bayes: Python

In this project, I investigated the relationship between left and right hippocampus volumes in individuals being tested for dementia. The goal of this was to use a training data set to then utilize Naive Bayes Theorem to predict whether a patient would develop dementia based on the volumes of their hippocampus.

The dataset was acquired from the given site: http://www.oasis-brains.org/.

Using the dataset above, I acquired a training and a testing set so that I would be able to determine the overall accuracy of my algorithm. I then determined the four different scenarios that could occur from the dataset provided, these scenarios being the likelihood that it is the left hippocampus volume given the patient is healthy or has dementia and the likelihood that it is the right hippocampus volume given the patient is healthy or has dementia. From here, I then calculated the gaussian probability density function for each scenario. I then calculated the probability that the patient in the test data set has dementia utilizing the training set and the Naive Bayes equation.

The overall accuracy of my written algorithm was 72.6%, meaning that given a dataset where it is unknown whether the individual has dementia or healthy given their left and right hippocampus volumes, my program would be able to identify who developed dementia 72.6% of the time.

Determination of Hypertension Using Contingency Tables: Python

For this assignment, I was tasked with determining whether men or women have a higher chance of developing hypertension. To approach this, given the population data, I decided to write a hypergeometric distribution function and utilize a contingency table to determine the different probabilities.

To begin with, I began by developing a null hypothesis, that both men and women have the same probability of developing hypertension. Utilizing pandas, seaborn, and numpy, I was then able to determine a p-value to compare to a significance value of .05.

The results depicted a p-value of .024. Because this was less than the significance value of 0.05, I rejected the null hypothesis and determined that men and women do not share the same chance of developing hypertension in that women actually have a greater chance of developing hypertension.

Electroenchephalogram Controlled Robotic Exoskeleton

For my senior capstone project, I decided to focus on developing an electroenchephologram (EEG) controlled rehabilitative robotic exoskeleton. This project was meant to aid patients with little to no muscle activation in their arms to start training their brain and muscles to rehabilitate and restore muscle activation. An EEG is a "helmet" device wired with electrodes that connect to the users head. This machine is then able to read in brain activity and output it as noisy voltage signals. Knowing this, I was able to pair this device with a machine learning algorithm and a pneumatic controlled exoskeleton to classify brain signals as three movements, "left", "right", and "stationary" in real-time. This means that if a patient is thinking of moving the noted arm to the right, left, or leaving it stationary, then the machine learning classifier algorithm will allow for the data to be read and separated into those sections, allowing for the exoskeleton to actuate to the left, right, or be held stationary automatically.

For the design of this exoskeleton, I opted to use a light weight design that is rated to hold the weight of an arm and is also able to have rollers installed on the base so that the device can roll given an actuation method. The actuation method in this case was using pneumatics. This was due to the resources that I was given, if possible, I would prefer to have used an electric motor with low audio outputs as any little change/distraction in the individuals environment could cause drastically different signals, making it difficult to classify a given movement in a machine learning model. This will be touched on later. The pneumatic was controlled using an air pump source and an Arduino Nano while the real-time classification was done using a RaspberryPi.

Below are images of the design, assembly, and circuit diagram of the system:

In order to classify movements from the noisy signals, I utilized a CNN algorithm with preprocessing filters and numerous layers being applied to the data. The two major preprocessing filters used in this case were the Sobel Kernel and the Gaussian Kernel. Sobel Kernel allowed for edge detection in the EEG data whereas the Gaussian kernel normalizes the data. The layers utilized were a Linear Sequential Model, Conv2D Layers, Batch Normalization Layers, Max Pooling Layers, Dropout Layers, Flatten Layers, and Dense Layers. For the activation function, due to its efficiency, we utilized the LeakyReLU function. All of these aspects of the code utilized in conjunction allowed for feature extraction and classification. After gathering a training set of over 100,000 data points (all from my own head) I was able to create a training and test set for classifier weight calculations. Given these weights, we can then use these weights during real-time EEG readings to classify the movements as the data is being collected.

Below is a GIF of the EEG working and the output on the RaspberryPi:

Overall, I was able to beat a random classification. Given that there were three possible movements, then purely random would be about 33%. However, my algorithm achieved an accuracy of 43%.

Self-Made Electric Guitar and Custom Guitar Pedals

For this project, I collaborated with a team of 5 engineers to develop an electric guitar and two pedals to pair. In this project, I assisted with the initial idea and design of this guitar using SolidWorks. Although I assisted in the design development, I was primarily focused on the circuit conceptualization and building of the two pedals.

Once the general concept was created, I spearheaded the design choice of having movable wings on the guitar that, when moved, affected the overall volume and tone of guitar. Given time restraints, this was not successfully achieved, however we were able to prototype the movable wing properly. This was done by setting up two interlocked gears at the base of each wing and connecting them within a hollow housing unit on the back of the guitar. This allowed the gears to be hidden from sight, while allowing for smooth movements of the wings.

Below are images of the design and final product of the guitar:

For the pedals, I decided to develop one known pedal and one custom pedal to suit the needs that my team and I determined. To begin with, I modeled and built an overdrive pedal on NI Multisim and a breadboard. The design is fairly simple, with 2 major components; the op-amp and the diodes. The op-amp in this case was wired in a non-inverting amplifier configuration which allowed for the maximization of gain to be possible. The diodes in this case were set-up to create clipping within the signal, allowing for the peaks of the signals to be flattened. By using two diodes in this configuration, it causes soft-clipping due to the fact that it is in the feedback loop of the op-amp. This means that the peaks of the signals are partially clipped, allowing for distortion to be present within the output.

Below are images of the overdrive circuit model, breadboard, and output waveform:

The custom pedal was aimed to mimic the sound of an engine starting in the beginning of the note being played to match the theme of the F-14 Tomcat. This could be done by maximizing the gain, as was done in the overdrive pedal, while hard clipping the top of the waveform. With this in mind, I was able to begin developing the circuit. For the first portion of the circuit, I had set up another op-amp in the non-inverting amplifier configuration, but this time I added a capacitor to the feedback loop of the amplifier. This essentially acts as a low pass filter, limiting higher frequencies while allowing lower frequencies to pass through. This reduces noise in the system. In addition, I implemented a diode at the output of the op-amp causing hard clipping. This essentially takes the peak signal and flattens it. The final component was another op-amp in the inverted configuration with a PNP transistor at the output of the circuit. This increased the overall current output and gradually decreased the voltage output. This circuit led to a popping/revving sound at the beginning of the note, while tapering off into distortion as the signal continued.

Car Window Mechanism Model

In order to simulate the minimum and maximum points, I utilized IR Distance sensors. To change the direction of the motor, I utilized a 4 relay system modeling an H-bridge circuit. Once this was done, I wanted to implement a pinch mechanism, so that in the case a finger or a hand gets stuck when the window was closing, then the window would stop and then begin to open once again.
